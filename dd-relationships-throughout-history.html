Relationships Throughout History

Marriage as we know it is the anomaly, not the baseline.

The Alliance Era

Start with the thing almost everyone gets backwards. For most of recorded history, a wedding was not two people deciding to build a life together. It was two families executing a contract, and the couple standing at the center of it were closer to the terms of the deal than the parties to it. Their job was to be joined, exchanged, and made to produce — an heir, an alliance, a claim on land. Whether they wanted each other was, at best, a secondary detail. Often it was not a detail anyone thought to write down.

One caveat before we go further, because it shapes everything that follows: this is a history of paperwork. We know about Mesopotamia, Rome, England, and colonial America because those societies wrote things down and the writing survived. So the story leans literate, propertied, and — after antiquity — heavily Western. That limit cuts in a specific direction: the couples who left records were disproportionately the ones with property worth contracting over. The further you get from the archive, the more the picture blurs, which is a reason to hold every generalization here a little loosely and to treat the societies that kept the best receipts as a sample, not a census.

And the contract was rarely even one man to one woman. We tend to picture the past as a long, tidy row of one-husband-one-wife households with the modern couple at the end of the line. Widen the lens past the written West, though, and the tidy row falls apart. The anthropological record says the human default was never so simple — and it says so at scale.

Record

When the anthropologist Murdock catalogued 1,231 societies in his Ethnographic Atlas (compiled 1960–1980), only 186 were strictly monogamous. 1,041 permitted some form of polygyny — a man taking several wives — and just four practiced polyandry. That is the source of the familiar line that roughly 85% of documented societies have allowed plural marriage. But the honest version is the more interesting one: even where polygyny was permitted, most actual marriages were still monogamous. Cross-cultural codes suggest only about 14% of men and 22% of women in those societies were ever polygynously married — a few high-status men held multiple wives while most men had one, or none. And it tracked power: the anthropologist Betzig found that the more despotic the state, the more wives its rulers piled up.

Source — G.P. Murdock, Ethnographic Atlas (n = 1,231); cross-cultural polygyny-prevalence codes; L. Betzig on despotism and reproductive monopoly.

The number people quote — roughly 85% of societies permitting plural marriage — sounds like a world of harems. It was not. The quieter figure buried inside it is the one that matters: even where the law let a man take several wives, most marriages were still one-to-one, because most men could neither afford nor command more than one. Polygyny, in practice, was a privilege of the top. A few high-status men absorbed extra wives; a corresponding tail of low-status men got none at all. Say that plainly and a pattern appears that outlasts every institution in this essay: the marriage market has always been stratified — sorted by rank, wealth, and power — and formal polygyny is simply what that stratification looks like when the law lets it show on the surface. Take the law away and the sorting does not vanish. It just goes quiet. Hold onto that shape. It disappears from the written record for most of this story, and then comes roaring back at the very end.

When marriage does surface in the written record, it surfaces as commerce. The oldest receipts we have are marriage contracts pressed into clay in Mesopotamia, and they read like exactly what they were: transfers of property with terms attached. The groom’s side paid a bride-price; the bride’s side brought a dowry; and the consent that made it binding belonged to the fathers, not to the couple. The reason it all had to be written down is the reason any high-value transaction is written down — so that ownership, obligation, and the legitimacy of any children could be proven later, in front of an authority. The Code of Hammurabi, around 1750 BC, made the logic brutally plain: a marriage without a contract simply did not count.

No tablet, no wife.

Record

Hammurabi’s code treated a broken engagement like a broken commercial deal. A suitor who backed out forfeited both his betrothal deposit and the bride-price he had already paid. A father-in-law who reneged had to repay the suitor double. And if a rival had lured the bride away, that father still paid double — and the rival was barred from marrying the daughter at all. The dowry, meanwhile, was the wife’s safeguard: her husband managed it during the marriage but had to return it if he divorced her.

Source — Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BC) and Mesopotamian marriage-contract scholarship (World History Encyclopedia).

Look at what those penalties are actually protecting. Not anyone’s feelings — the deal. A betrothal was a contract with money down, and breaking it triggered the same kind of remedy a broken commercial agreement would: forfeited deposits, double repayment, the offending party locked out of the market. The dowry rule points the same way. It existed to keep a divorced wife from being left destitute, yes, but also to stop her family’s capital from being quietly absorbed into her husband’s line. Marriage here is infrastructure for moving property and producing legitimate heirs, and the law is the enforcement layer that keeps the infrastructure honest.

Which brings us to the thread that runs through every era in this essay, so it is worth naming now, at the very start. A husband could divorce a wife who bore no children — but he had to return her dowry to do it, which made divorce expensive, so taking a concubine was frequently the cheaper move (and infertility, needless to say, was always blamed on the woman). Notice what is being managed there. It is not sex, and it is certainly not love. It is the consequence of sex: the legitimate child, and the property and paternity that ride on it. Almost no society on this timeline regulates desire directly. What they regulate, relentlessly, is what desire produces. Consequences policed, not sex — that is the spine of the whole story, and it begins here, in clay.

Rome ran the identical logic through a far more elaborate legal machine. The stated purpose of marriage was children; the legal minimum ages were twelve for girls and fourteen for boys; and a valid union required the consent of the couple and their fathers. In practice most women married between fifteen and twenty and men in their late twenties, with elite families reaching for earlier matches to lock in dynastic alliances before a rival could. The Romans even split marriage into two legal flavors: cum manu, which moved the wife and her property into her husband’s control, and sine manu, which left both with her birth family. Sine manu won out over time — and the reason it won tells you what the institution was really for. Families preferred the version that kept their capital from leaking into the husband’s line. When the deciding question in a marriage is which household keeps the assets, you are not looking at a love story. You are looking at a merger.

And Rome pushed the logic to a place that feels startlingly modern: it made marriage a matter of state. In 18 BC the emperor Augustus’s Lex Iulia required men to marry by twenty-five and women by twenty, penalized the unmarried, let childless widows forfeit half of what they stood to inherit, and rewarded the parents of several children with preference for public office. Read that as more than a list of rules. It is a state treating marriage as infrastructure — something the polity needs built and maintained at a certain rate, like roads or granaries — and treating the single, childless citizen as a policy problem to be corrected. The lever it reached for is the same one every earlier line of this section has been about: inheritance. Rome did not try to make people want to marry. It made not marrying, and not producing heirs, expensive — because the thing the state actually cared about, once again, was not who desired whom, but what the next generation would look like and who would inherit.

There was hard demography underneath the moralizing. Childbirth killed women of prime marrying age often enough that, from about the second century BC, Rome faced a chronic shortage of marriageable, fertile women. In cold market terms, a proven childbearer was the scarcest asset around, which pushed divorce and remarriage upward as men competed for the women most likely to give them surviving heirs. The alliance market was not sentimental about its own churn. It priced people — including for the risk that they might die producing the very heirs they were valued for.

Framework

Read the alliance era through The Love Equations and it looks like a sexual marketplace running one level up from the individual: two households appraising each other’s assets and settling at rough parity, with children as the contract’s deliverable. On this lens, what we now call hypergamy — the pull toward a higher-status partner — is a fossil. The reflex to trade up is the residue of an age when marrying up was a literal negotiation between families, conducted in bride-price and dowry, with a family’s rank on the line. The instinct outlived the institution that once made it explicit.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

For all its machinery, the alliance system had one conspicuous blind spot: nowhere in it did the wishes of the two people being married actually count for much. That is the gap the next thousand years would begin — slowly, unevenly, and almost by accident — to close.

The Church Rewrites the Rules

When the Roman order gave way to the medieval Church, the institution inherited the alliance system intact — and then, over centuries, began quietly editing it. The Church’s great and strange innovation was a single requirement: consent. By most accounts it spent generations insisting that what actually made a marriage was the freely given “I do” of the two people themselves — not the father’s signature, not the transfer of property. In everyday practice, families went on arranging and pressuring exactly as before, and a peasant girl’s “free” consent was rarely very free. But the principle, once written into doctrine, was radical and load-bearing. For the first time in this story the individual was standing inside the institution rather than beside it as one of its terms. The Church is also generally credited with tightening the rules against marrying close kin, which historians argue helped dissolve the very clan networks that alliance-marriage had been built to serve — though the mechanics of that are debated. Either way, a wedge had been driven between the family’s interest and the couple’s, and it would never fully close again.

It is tempting to read that as the moment love entered marriage. It was not — and the distinction matters enough to slow down on, because it is the hinge the whole essay turns on. The consent revolution did not invent love. The mistake buried in the assumption that it did — that people in the past simply did not feel what we feel — collapses the instant anyone goes looking for the evidence.

Record

When the anthropologists Jankowiak and Fischer searched the Standard Cross-Cultural Sample for romantic love — love songs, elopements, the longing and anguish of infatuation — they found unmistakable evidence of it in 147 of 166 cultures, or 88.5%. A later re-analysis pushed the figure to around 92%. Passionate love is not a Western invention, and not a modern one; it looks very close to a human universal.

Source — Jankowiak & Fischer (1992), 166 cultures in the Standard Cross-Cultural Sample; follow-up Jankowiak & Paladino (2008).

Love was never the anomaly. Marrying because of it was.

So here is the distinction, stated as plainly as it can be. Romantic love is ancient and very nearly everywhere. What is historically strange — the actual anomaly this essay is chasing — is the idea that love should be the thing that triggers a marriage. For almost all of the past, people fell in love constantly and married for entirely other reasons: land, alliance, labor, security, the wishes of their parents. Love and marriage were two systems that occasionally overlapped, not one system with two names. And the proof is hiding inside the very list of symptoms the anthropologists used to find love in the first place: elopement. Almost every culture that felt romantic love also kept a recognized category — a word, and usually a punishment — for running off with the person you loved instead of the person you were supposed to marry. You do not need such a category unless love and marriage routinely point in different directions. The category is the fingerprint of the gap.

Because love and marriage were separate systems, the marriages themselves could be coldly practical about things like age — which is where the period’s most lurid reputation comes from, and where most of it turns out to be wrong. The stock image is the medieval child bride, married off at twelve. It barely survives contact with the records. Medieval historians find that the genuinely young brides were royal women, wed in their early-to-mid teens for reasons of state, precisely because their marriages were the highest-stakes alliances of all. Ordinary Italian and English town girls married in their mid-twenties; peasant girls in their early twenties. The dramatic older-man, younger-girl pairing we picture was largely an Italian pattern, not an English one — a quiet joke buried in the Western canon, because the most famous teenage marriage in our literature is Romeo and Juliet’s, and Shakespeare set it in Verona. In England it would have raised eyebrows. And when English marriage certificates finally start surviving in bulk, they settle the argument outright: of a thousand recorded in the archdiocese of Canterbury between 1619 and 1660, exactly one bride was thirteen, and 966 of the thousand were nineteen or older at their first marriage.

That raises the obvious question — if not children being married off young, then why were Europeans marrying so late? The answer is one of the most influential ideas in the history of the family, and also one of the most contested: the Hajnal line. Draw a rough diagonal across the map from Trieste to Leningrad. West of it, the demographer Hajnal argued, sat a marriage pattern unlike most of the world’s — women marrying in their mid-twenties, men a few years older, a tenth to a fifth of people never marrying at all, and newlyweds expected to form a brand-new household rather than move in under a parent’s roof. East of the line, marriage came early and nearly universally, with young couples folded into large multi-generational families.

Record

West of the Hajnal line, roughly half of women aged fifteen to fifty were married at any given moment; east of it, closer to 70%. The pattern is real and important — but honesty about the evidence matters here: Hajnal’s thesis has been repeatedly revised and criticized by twenty-first-century scholars, partly because it leaned on thin or underused Eastern European data. Treat it as an influential map of the terrain, not a settled measurement.

Source — J. Hajnal on European marriage patterns; reconstructions via the Cambridge Group for the History of Population, with significant later revision.

The engine behind that late Western marriage was money, not romance. Forming an independent household — the thing Western marriage now required — took years of saving, usually earned through a long stint in service on someone else’s farm or in someone else’s house before a couple could afford a home of their own. Marriage was an economic threshold you crossed when you could finally pay for it, which is why so many crossed it late and some never crossed it at all. Love was not what set the clock. It was present the whole time — the songs and the elopements prove it — it simply was not in charge. And the thread from the alliance era runs straight through this one: the Church’s entire apparatus of public vows, witnesses, and recorded legitimacy existed to make sure that when a child arrived, everyone knew which marriage it belonged to. Consent was the genuinely new ingredient. Controlling the consequences of sex was still the point.

The couple now had a foot in the door. What they did not yet have was a marriage built around their own happiness — or a culture willing to be honest about what they were already doing in the meantime. Both were on their way.

The Early Modern Turn

If the Middle Ages carry an undeserved reputation for child brides, the early modern centuries carry one for prudishness — the buttoned-up world of stern sermons and unbroken chastity before the wedding. It is just as false. In the rural English parishes of the late sixteenth century, a startling share of women walked to the altar already visibly pregnant — by one careful count of the parish registers, roughly a quarter of all brides (the demographer P.E.H. Hair’s work). This was not a scandal grimly tolerated; for courting couples headed toward marriage it was close to routine. Premarital sex, in other words, carried little real shame on its own. What carried shame — and what the community and its courts actually moved against — was its consequence arriving in the wrong order: a child with no marriage behind it, and therefore no one clearly on the hook to support it.

The court records make the logic almost comically explicit. Elizabethan church courts routinely hauled in couples who were already married and fined them for “antenuptial incontinence” — the offense of having conceived a child before the very wedding they had gone on to hold. Meanwhile couples who were merely courting, and clearly headed for marriage, were frequently left entirely alone. Sit with how strange that is by modern lights: the system reached past the unmarried lovers to punish the married ones, retroactively, for the timing of a pregnancy. It only makes sense once you see what was being policed. It was never the act of sex. It was whether a binding contract stood behind the child — and if the contract eventually arrived, the court still docked you for having collected on it early.

Framework

This is the through-line of the whole essay stated inside a single institution, and The Love Equations reads it as the real constant across every era on the timeline: societies almost never regulate sex as such. They regulate its consequences — who owes whom a child, whose household that child belongs to, and whose property it can one day claim. Seen this way, premarital sex on the engagement track was just the contract being consummated a little early; the only true crime was a consequence with no contract attached to it. Hammurabi’s dowry rules, Rome’s obsession with legitimate heirs, and an Elizabethan bridal-pregnancy fine are all the same machine wearing different clothes.

Every age polices the consequences of sex — not the sex.

And if you want the single sharpest proof that the “puritanical past” is a fantasy we invented later, look directly at the people we named the fantasy after: the actual Puritans of colonial New England. They practiced bundling. A courting couple, unmarried, would be permitted to spend the entire night in bed together, under the same roof as the girl’s parents, who considered it a sensible way for two young people to get to know each other through a long, cold night. The pilgrims were, in effect, hosting supervised sleepovers.

Record

By the late 1700s, an estimated 30 to 40% of New England brides were pregnant at marriage. Bundling was a recognized courtship practice, and a resulting pregnancy carried a clear expectation: the couple would marry. Puritan New England, in other words, out-scandalized the myth we tell about it.

Source — colonial New England marriage and birth records; studies of the bundling custom.

Underneath the sex, though, the shape of marriage itself was slowly changing, and that is the real story of the period. The old late-marriage pattern still held — women marrying in their mid-twenties, men a couple of years older, four or five children over a lifetime, a sizeable minority never marrying at all. But the unions now forming increasingly looked companionate: grounded in the couple’s own consent, close in age, light on parental control, and set up in a household of their own. Marriage was inching toward being a partnership rather than a merger. The law, characteristically, lagged the feeling — and not only on timing. By most accounts English common law still ran on coverture, the doctrine under which a wife’s legal identity was largely folded into her husband’s: she could not readily own property, sign contracts, or keep her own wages. So a marriage in this period could be a genuine love match and a legal erasure in the same breath — the woman choosing her partner freely and, in the eyes of the law, half-disappearing the instant she did. The state was slow even to acknowledge the couple’s new power: clandestine, consent-only marriages were not actually outlawed in England until Hardwicke’s Marriage Act of 1754, and in Scotland not until 1856 — which is the whole reason eloping English couples famously bolted north to Gretna Green.

There is one more feature of these marriages that the modern eye misses entirely, because we measure marriage against a lifespan our ancestors did not have. These unions ended sooner than the vow implied — routinely, and not by divorce, which was nearly impossible for ordinary people, but by death. And death churned the marriage market in almost exactly the way divorce does now.

Record

Serial monogamy is not a modern invention; only the exit mechanism changed. Between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries, roughly 30% of all marriages in France involved a widow or widower. In the eighteenth century, about half of widowers and a third of widows remarried. In nineteenth-century Flanders, remarriages made up 20–25% of all marriages in the first half of the century, falling to 10–15% by its end as lifespans lengthened. “Till death do us part” was a far shorter contract than it sounds.

Source — French and Flemish parish-register demography, 16th–19th centuries.

The same demographic pressure could, tellingly, produce the opposite institution. In China, by the historical scholarship, widow remarriage was progressively restricted from the Sui dynasty onward, and the Ming and Qing states went further, granting public honors — commemorative chastity arches — to widows who refused to remarry at all. Same underlying shortage of surviving spouses; opposite official answer, one culture recycling its widows briskly back into the market and the other building monuments to those who stayed out of it. Both, notice, are ways of managing the consequences bound up in a marriage — property, lineage, obligation — rather than anything to do with the widow’s own desire. And underneath every version of it sat the children, who in most pre-modern economies were labor and old-age insurance as much as they were kin. With child mortality staggeringly high by our standards, families by most accounts had to over-produce births simply to see a few survive to adulthood — which kept fertility, not affection, sitting quietly at the center of the arrangement, even as affection was, for the first time, being invited to the table.

Companionate marriage had arrived in feeling. What it still lacked was the one thing that could make it fully voluntary: a way for people to survive, and even thrive, without marrying at all. That was about to be manufactured — in factories.

The Love-Match Revolution

Then the ground moved, and it moved because of work. Industrialization pulled production out of the household and into the factory and the office, and in doing so it quietly dismantled the economic logic that had underwritten marriage for five thousand years. For all of recorded history a household had been a unit of production — a farm, a workshop, a going concern that a couple and their children ran together and that neither could easily run alone. Marriage was, among other things, how you staffed it. Wage labor broke that. Once a person could earn a living as an individual, selling their hours to an employer, the household stopped being a business you needed a spouse to operate. Marriage did not disappear, but its footing shifted underneath it: it stopped being an economic necessity and became something far more precarious for an institution — a choice. And what the new economy offered on top of the wage was the thing that had always been present but never in command. People could now marry for love, because they no longer strictly had to marry for anything else. The breadwinner model rose to fill the gap, carrying forward the asymmetry that, by most accounts, coverture had long encoded — the husband now the earner rather than the legal owner — and a genuinely new expectation crept in behind it. A marriage was now supposed to make you happy. Happiness had been promoted from a lucky accident to a job requirement.

A wage made marriage optional. And optional is fragile.

You can watch the old rules and the new reality pull completely apart in the numbers of the period.

Record

English law still permitted marriage at fourteen for boys and twelve for girls. Yet by the late nineteenth century the average couple married at roughly 26.5 (men) and 25 (women), with women’s marriage age varying measurably by social class. The legal floor and actual behavior had, by this point, nothing to do with each other.

Source — English marriage law and late-nineteenth-century demographic averages.

Read that gap for a moment. The law still carried floors written for a world of dynastic alliances, where marrying off a fourteen-year-old could make cold sense; the behavior on the ground belonged to a different civilization entirely, one where people waited until their mid-twenties because marriage had become a personal decision about a life, not a family’s move on a board. The floor and the practice had simply stopped speaking to each other.

What actually reshaped courtship in this stretch was, of all things, the automobile. Kinsey’s data caught the change mid-motion: American women who came of age in the 1920s were markedly less likely to be virgins at marriage than the cohort just before the First World War. But the shift was smaller and more orderly than the myth of the wild Jazz Age suggests — a clear majority of women under thirty were still virgins when they married, and of those who were not, about half had slept only with the man they went on to marry. The car did not detonate chastity; it privatized courtship. By most accounts it lifted young couples out from under the chaperone’s eye and handed them a room on wheels, and the older Victorian machinery of illegitimacy and reputation loosened along with it, slowly and unevenly. Even here the constant holds: what was relaxing was not primarily the rule against sex but the surveillance of its consequences, and it relaxed at roughly the speed those consequences came to feel survivable.

Which sets up the final act. Marriage had become a choice, made for love and judged by happiness — the most fragile basis it had ever rested on. All that remained was to sever the last hard link between sex and its consequences, and let the whole bundle fall apart into its pieces.

The Great Unbundling

Here is the punchline the entire timeline has been building toward, and it is worth stating bluntly, because so much modern argument is built on getting it backwards. The version of marriage that nostalgia treats as timeless — the young couple, the single male breadwinner, the house full of children, the wife at home — was not the baseline of history. It was a brief, freakish dip. American first-marriage ages fell to the lowest point in the country’s recorded history around the middle of the twentieth century — women marrying at roughly twenty, men around twenty-three, by most accounts — before the long climb back up to today’s late twenties and thirties. Everything this essay has walked through says the opposite is closer to the human norm: the mid-twenties brides of medieval England, the late-marrying Hajnal West, the companionate couples saving for years before they could afford a household. Late, deliberate, economically-gated marriage is the pattern. The “traditional” 1950s marriage that a certain kind of politics wants to restore was, measured against the whole timeline, the least traditional moment on it.

The 1950s were the least traditional moment on the timeline.

And then it came apart, fast. Everything marriage had once bundled into a single package — sex, living together, childbearing, economic partnership, social status — unbundled into separate stages that can now be taken in any order, with different people, or skipped entirely. The rough machinery of that unbundling is familiar: the arrival of the contraceptive pill around 1960, no-fault divorce spreading outward from California at the end of that decade, cohabitation sliding within a generation from scandal to unremarkable first step, and online dating rising to become the single most common way couples now meet. The exact figures are a job for another pass and are hedged here on purpose, but the direction is not seriously in doubt — by most measures the marriage rate has roughly halved since the early 1970s. Each of those changes looks like a separate social trend. They are really one event seen from different angles: the package coming apart into its parts.

And it is worth seeing precisely what the pill did, because it closes the loop this essay opened in clay. Every era we have walked through policed the consequences of sex far more than sex itself — Hammurabi’s rules about dowries and paternity, Rome legislating heirs, the Elizabethan court fining a pregnancy rather than a passion, the shotgun logic humming quietly under a New England bundling bed. The entire five-thousand-year apparatus existed to manage one thing: the child that sex might produce, and the property, paternity, and obligation that child dragged behind it. Reliable contraception severed sex from pregnancy — and in cutting that one thread, it quietly pulled the load-bearing wall out of the whole structure. Once sex need not produce a child, most of the machinery ever built around it — the contracts, the dowries, the legitimacy courts, the chastity arches — suddenly had nothing left to hold up. We are still standing in the room after the wall came out, arguing about what the space is now for.

And remember the shape from the very first section — the stratified market that formal polygyny once made visible, a handful of men at the top with a surplus and a long tail at the bottom with none. It never actually went anywhere. Monogamous marriage, near-universal and socially enforced, was in effect a system for spreading pairing more evenly across the population than raw desire would — a way of steering most men toward a wife and most women toward a household. Strip those enforcing institutions away, as the unbundling has, and the older shape simply resurfaces: an open market where attention and options pool around a minority at the top, exactly as they pooled when the pooling was written into polygynous law. It is, in a strange way, polygyny’s underlying math reasserting itself without polygyny’s rules — which is why a growing permanent-single class is not really a glitch in the modern system. It is the system’s default setting, showing through now that so little is laid over the top of it.

Framework

So here is the lens The Love Equations leaves you holding. The modern love-match asks one person to be everything that three whole institutions — kin, church, and economy — used to divide among themselves. Your spouse is now meant to be your alliance, your moral compass, and your economic partner, and on top of all that the lover, best friend, and source of self-actualization no earlier age would have dreamed of demanding from a marriage. The market did not get harsher. The job description got longer — far longer than any single relationship has ever been asked to carry.

Which reframes the anxiety of modern dating from the ground up. It is not evidence that something has broken. It is the predictable cost of running the most ambitious, and by a wide margin the least supported, experiment in human pairing ever attempted — two people trying to be, for each other, everything an entire village once provided. Seen against five thousand years of marriage as contract, alliance, and infrastructure, the surprising thing is not that it so often strains. It is that we expect it to bear the weight at all.
