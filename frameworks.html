Long-term partner selection gate
The 7–7 Rule
A binary qualification gate for serious long-term candidates: physical attractiveness ≥ 7 and the personality composite ≥ 7. The two sides are scored independently — a high score on one never compensates for a low score on the other. The personality side is an aggregate, not a single trait, so one charming quality can’t carry a weak overall composite. Enforced live in the Compatibility Calculator.
Physical attractiveness
≥ 7
The looks side of the gate — a single factor. Sustained physical attraction is non-negotiable for long-term viability; below the threshold, desire degrades over time.
Below 7 → insufficient sustained attraction risk
Personality composite
≥ 7
The aggregate of the personality factors — classiness, warmth, assertiveness, character, shared values, and fit — averaged into one score. One standout trait doesn’t lift a weak composite.
Composite below 7 → insufficient long-term compatibility risk
Qualifies
Looks ≥ 7 AND personality composite ≥ 7
Both sides clear. Long-term candidacy is open.
Does not qualify
Either side falls below 7
Long-term candidacy fails. One weak side disqualifies regardless of the other.
Example logic

A person can hold an 8 personality composite and still fail long-term candidacy if physical attraction is a 6. And a single charming trait — say a 9 in humor — doesn’t rescue a composite that everything else has dragged to a 6. The rule blocks rationalized compromise on either side: sustained desire and a genuinely good overall personality both have to independently clear 7.

Reasoning

The point of the 7–7 Rule is not perfectionism. It is preventing a relationship from being built on a missing pillar. Sustained desire and sustained personality fit both have to be independently good enough.

Hard floor gate
Sub-5 Auto Disqualification
A score below 5 in any single factor — physical, character, or fit — signals active incompatibility, not just weakness. It doesn’t matter how strong everything else is: one factor under 5 trips the floor. Unlike the 7–7 Rule, this is per-metric, not an aggregate — and it’s checked first, before the 7–7 evaluation. Enforced live in the Compatibility Calculator.
Any physical factor
< 5
Attractiveness, fitness, presentation — if any one of them drops below 5, sustained desire cannot be reasonably expected.
Any character / fit factor
< 5
Classiness, stability, values, availability — if any one of them drops below 5, the dynamic itself becomes the problem.
Reasoning
Active incompatibility. Sub-5 is not neutral — it is a negative signal that requires constant tolerance, wherever it shows up.
Any single axis can trip it. The floor applies to every factor independently — not to an overall average. One sub-5 metric is enough.
No compensation. Strength elsewhere can’t offset a sub-5 factor; the weak point sets the ceiling for the whole match.

Treatment Markup System

Market structure model
SMV Matching
How attractiveness sorts the market. Score looks as a within-sex percentile — a 7 is the 70th percentile of her own sex, not an absolute. Across real couples, partners’ rated looks correlate at r ≈ 0.4 Tier 1 (Feingold’s meta-analysis; a 2024 dyadic re-analysis confirms it) — strong matching, well short of identical. Two things follow: a Matching Curve that pulls every pairing toward the middle, and an Option Pool that is wildly asymmetric by sex. One correction up front: on looks alone, no one trades up — not even women. The “trading up” everyone notices is a status trade, and it lives in the later layers, not here.

Subcategory 2 · optionality
The Option Pool
Who’s available to you — not who you end up with. Options and pairings are different things.
Options are asymmetric; pairings are symmetric. Women drown in inbound interest, men starve: 57% of men who online-dated said they got too few messages, while women were 5× more likely to say too many, and 54% of women felt overwhelmed by the volume Tier 1 (Pew). For men the pool is also top-heavy — Tinder “like” inequality runs a Gini ~0.58 Tier 3. Critical caveat: that measures swipes in a free, looks-only context — interest, not couples, and wildly overstates real pairing, which still regresses to matching. The famous “bottom 80% of men” line is swipe data, not relationships.

Both then pair by matching (Subcategory 1) — which is why she often feels like she pulled up: the higher-looks man beside her is usually compensating with status. Supply ≈ one decile per tier; 10s are the top sliver.
Why you date within your pool, not at the top of it
“The league above is intimidating” — real. Remove rejection risk in the lab and people do chase the most attractive (Huston, 1973); under normal risk they aim at matched targets. And the tier above has better options, so it regresses to its match and genuinely reciprocates less. Tier 2
“Matching is comfortable” — real. Matched pairs carry less mate-guarding anxiety, and couples who knew each other longer before dating match less on looks (Hunt, Eastwick & Finkel, 2015) — familiarity lets other value override the looks market. Tier 2
“Below feels beneath me” — partly. A partner’s looks signal your own mate value, so dating well down carries a real cost — but mostly people decline down simply because they can usually get matched, and matched beats settling. Tier 2
Supply does the quiet work. The top of your pool is scarce and contested; the middle is abundant. Even with access to a few above you, you simply meet far more near the middle — so you land where the mass is. Tier 1
