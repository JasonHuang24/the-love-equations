Third Spaces

The apps didn’t win the courtship market. The places where people met were demolished first.

The Well and the Agora

Every settlement that has ever existed needed a place to draw water, and so every settlement had a place where, sooner or later, everyone turned up. The well, the spring, the public fountain: the young came to them alongside the old, because water admitted no exceptions, and in the coming and going a good deal of looking got done. Nobody built a well to make marriages. It made them anyway, by gathering people who had nowhere else they were all obliged to be.

Beside the accidental gathering places stood the deliberate ones. The Greek agora and the Roman forum were built for the business of the city — trade, law, argument, worship — and assembled the whole town in one square as a matter of routine. The baths, the market days, the great religious festivals did the same on their own calendars. None of these existed to introduce anyone to anyone. They did it constantly all the same, as a side effect of gathering the whole population into one place at one time.

One limit shapes the whole of this early stretch, and it belongs stated at the outset. What survives from antiquity is the record of the literate city — Athens, Rome, the propertied Mediterranean — and almost nothing of the countless villages whose gathering places generated no text at all. The picture that follows leans urban and Western for the same reason every picture of the deep past does: that is where the writing was kept. Read the well and the square described here as a legible sample of a very old and very widespread human habit, not a census of it.

Before the swipe, there was the square.

Within those spaces courtship was rarely private and never quite free. Many of the gathering places were segregated by sex or watched by kin, and a young woman at the well or the festival moved inside a web of relatives, neighbors, and reputation that did the work a dating profile does now, vouching for her and constraining her in the same motion. Meeting someone meant being seen meeting them. The community was not a backdrop to courtship. It was the medium courtship traveled through, and there was no route around it.

The one Roman who wrote the market down as a market was Ovid. His Ars Amatoria, published around 2 AD, is a seduction manual whose entire premise is that the city itself is the venue — that a man in search of a woman need only learn which shared spaces reliably held them. He sends his pupil to the colonnades of Pompey’s portico, to the gallery Livia built, to the temples, to the law courts, and above all to the theatre and the Circus, where the crowd pressed close enough to make conversation easy and retreat hard. He is entirely unsentimental about what the places are for.

He was describing the theatre, which he named elsewhere as the place most dangerous to a woman’s chastity — not as a warning but as a recommendation. Two thousand years before anyone spoke of a marketplace of partners, a Roman poet had noticed that the city’s shared rooms were where pairing happened, and had written the guidebook. The venues were not designed for courtship; they gathered people for water, law, and spectacle, and courtship happened in the margins of all of it.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, the well and the agora are the first instance of the claim this whole history turns on: pairing is downstream of where people are gathered, not of how much they want to pair. The “market” for partners has always been a physical place before it was anything else — a well, a portico, a festival — and on this lens the surest way to change who pairs with whom is not to change what people want but to change the rooms they stand in. Move the venue and the market moves with it, farther than any shift in desire ever could.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

Parish and Fair

When the classical city receded, its gathering places did not vanish so much as change management. For the thousand years between the fall of Rome and the Reformation, the two dependable venues of ordinary European life were the parish church and the seasonal fair, and between them they set the rhythm of courtship for most of the population that ever lived.

The church did something no other institution of the medieval world reliably did: it gathered the same faces, every week, within walking distance. Sunday assembled a village’s whole eligible population in one building on a fixed schedule, then did it again seven days later, and again after that. For a young person, mass and the churchyard around it were very likely the largest recurring pool of marriageable near-strangers they would ever stand in. The companion history to this one, on the Church era, traces how the same institution rewrote the rules of consent from the inside. As a venue its role was plainer and more physical: it was the one room that reliably filled, on schedule, with the whole eligible parish.

The fair supplied the opposite pool. A few times a year the roads filled and a much larger, stranger crowd assembled for a market, a patronal feast, or a hiring fair where young servants changed households for the coming year and, not by coincidence, met people from beyond their own parish. Between the weekly draw of the church and the periodic surge of the fair, a courting population had both a reliable local circle and an occasional wider one. What it did not have was much say over the timing.

The calendar decided when you could meet.

People paired on the schedule the parish and the fair provided, clustering around the fixed points of the year when the venues happened to open — a courtship season, more or less, set by the liturgical and agricultural clock rather than by anyone’s choosing. Precise numbers for any of this are thin, and the honest position is to hold the picture loosely: the specifics of who met whom at a fifteenth-century fair are mostly lost. The structure is legible enough, though. This was a world that did not lack places to meet so much as ration them, opening its venues on holy days and market days and closing them the rest of the time.

The Coffeehouse Century

Then something genuinely new appeared: a gathering place you paid a small coin to enter, built for no purpose but company itself. The coffeehouse reached England in the 1650s and multiplied fast, and around it grew a whole ecology of commercial sociability — the tavern and the public house, the assembly room, the pleasure garden, the salon. For the first time the venue was not an accident of water or worship or trade but a business whose product was the company of other people, sold by the hour.

Samuel Pepys, a young naval official with an appetite for everything London offered, recorded his first visit to one of these rooms in his diary. It was December 1660, the coffeehouse stood in Cornhill, and what struck him was exactly the quality the places were selling.

Diversity of company and discourse: without a word of theory, Pepys had put his finger on what would later be given a name. A place you visit voluntarily, again and again, for the pleasure of mixing with people you do not already know — he set the definition down in a diary entry three centuries before anyone thought to define it. The coffeehouses thickened public life so completely that whole institutions grew up inside them.

Record

London’s coffeehouses were nicknamed penny universities: a penny bought a dish of coffee and, with it, entry to hours of news, argument, and instruction from whoever else was in the room. Serious commerce incubated at these tables. Modern insurance grew out of Edward Lloyd’s coffeehouse, where shipping men gathered to trade risk — the house gave its name to Lloyd’s of London. And the city’s stockbrokers ran their business from coffeehouses like Jonathan’s and Garraway’s in Exchange Alley for over a century before they built themselves a dedicated Stock Exchange.

Source — histories of the London coffeehouse (the “penny universities”; Lloyd’s of London; the Exchange Alley coffeehouses).

A room whose only product was company.

An honesty problem cuts against the tidy story here. The flagship third places of the coffeehouse century were, overwhelmingly, male preserves. A respectable woman did not spend her afternoons in a Cornhill coffeehouse trading gossip and stock tips, and the rooms that were thickening public life were mostly thickening it for men. The era’s great expansion of sociability was not, or not yet, an expansion of the courtship market.

Courtship in the period kept its own venues, and they were more formal ones. It happened at balls and in the assembly rooms of towns like Bath, where a lady carried a dance card and a gentleman could not simply approach her — he had to be formally introduced before he could ask to be added to it. It happened at church socials, on the Sunday promenade, at the supervised edges of family life. These were gathering places too, but rationed and chaperoned ones, a world away from the open door of the coffeehouse. The commercial third space and the courtship space ran side by side through the same century without yet being the same place.

The Golden Age of Joining

By the last decades of the nineteenth century the gathering place had multiplied into something like a dense mesh laid over ordinary life. This was the golden age of joining: lodges and mutual-aid societies, unions and congregations, choirs and clubs and bowling leagues and fraternal orders, and above all the new commercial palaces of leisure — the dance hall, the department store, the amusement park. People did not merely visit these places; they belonged to them, by name and by subscription, and a population organized into overlapping memberships is a population continually being introduced to itself.

A joined society is one forever introducing itself.

The dance hall shows the shift in miniature. The earliest dances a young person attended were sponsored affairs — put on by a mutual-aid society or a fraternal order, held under the eyes of parents and organizers. What made the commercial dance hall of the 1890s so prized by working-class and immigrant youth was precisely what those sponsored dances lacked: no chaperones. Young people began running their own unchaperoned dances, “rackets,” where the supervision that had governed courtship for centuries simply was not present. The venue had quietly changed hands, from the community to the couple.

Record

Britain built the same appetite into architecture. The Hammersmith Palais de Danse opened in 1919 and lent its name to an era of vast commercial dance halls, the palais, that drew enormous crowds through the 1920s, ’30s, ’40s and ’50s. Just how many couples they made is hard to pin down: one much-repeated estimate, from the genealogy press rather than a peer-reviewed study, holds that in the 1950s as many as 70% of British couples first met at a dance hall. Treat the exact figure with caution. The direction it points — the dance hall as the mid-century’s dominant courtship venue — is not in serious doubt.

Source — Hammersmith Palais opened 1919; the “70% met at a dance hall” 1950s figure is a widely cited genealogy-press estimate, not a peer-reviewed one.

The same decades pulled courtship out of the American home. The historian Beth Bailey called it the move from front porch to back seat: a young man had once “called” on a woman in her family’s parlor, under her mother’s eye, but by the 1920s and ’30s couples increasingly “went out” — to the dance hall, the cinema, the restaurant, the car — beyond the household’s reach entirely. Part of what forced the change was simple lack of space: working women in cramped city apartments had no parlor in which to receive a caller, so courtship went public partly because the private venue had run out.

And the new venues were not only places of leisure. The workplace itself — the office, the shop floor, and above all the great department stores — became a courtship venue in its own right, watched over by a whole community of coworkers.

The store printed that in its own newsletter, for the whole staff to read. A courtship at the glove counter was not a private matter to be kept from the employer; it was company news, and the company cheered it on by name. Something in the small item is easy to miss: the match was public, vouched for, and embedded in a community that knew both parties and was frankly delighted for them. That texture — the crowd of familiars quietly rooting for a pairing — is one the coming century would strip out almost entirely.

Record

For most of the twentieth century, before the internet, couples met through a spread of overlapping social worlds with no single one dominant. In Michael Rosenfeld’s long reconstruction of how American couples met, friends were the leading route, climbing toward a third of all couples by the 1980s and ’90s, with family a large share in the earlier decades and meaningful contributions from school (which peaked near 21% around 1940), coworkers, the bar or restaurant, neighbors (holding around 10% at mid-century), and the church. The revealing thing is the shape of the whole list: local, face-to-face, and socially vouched almost all the way down. You met people through other people who already knew you both.

Source — Rosenfeld, Thomas & Hausen, How Couples Meet and Stay Together (PNAS 2019); mid-century shares are approximate, read from the study’s smoothed trend curves and peaking in different decades.

That portfolio was the golden age’s real achievement, though nobody experienced it as one. A person did not have to try to meet anyone; the meeting happened as a by-product of belonging — to a parish, a union, a workplace, a neighborhood, a dance hall on a Friday night. The introductions were ambient, produced for free by a society dense with places to stand near other people. And because the whole apparatus was invisible, its dismantling would be invisible too, taken for anything but a courtship problem until long after the venues were gone.

The Great Emptying

The turn comes around 1960, and it is worth stating flatly: the third space drained. Suburbanization spread people thin across the map; the car privatized the journey that used to throw strangers together; the television, and then the screen in every pocket, privatized the evening. Membership in the joined institutions fell, decade over decade, in nearly every category at once. The dense mesh that had done a century of ambient matchmaking thinned to almost nothing, and it thinned first — quietly, years before anyone connected it to why pairing had started to feel so hard.

Record

The numbers are stark across every kind of membership, and Robert Putnam assembled them in Bowling Alone: between 1980 and 1993 the number of Americans who bowled rose about 10% while league bowling fell 40%; Freemasonry was down 39% from its 1959 peak, the Jaycees down 44% and the Shriners down 27% since 1979; union membership slid from 32.5% of the non-farm workforce in 1953 to 15.8% by 1992; Red Cross volunteers fell 61% after 1970. Across the board, the average American belonged to roughly a quarter fewer associations than a generation earlier. Religious membership followed the same curve: Gallup found church, synagogue, and mosque membership steady near 70% for six decades, then falling from 70% in 1999 to 61% in 2010 and 47% in 2020 — below half for the first time in eight decades of the measure.

Source — R.D. Putnam, Bowling Alone (2000); Gallup, U.S. religious membership, 1937–2020.

The religious decline has a generational shape that shows where it is heading: membership runs at about 66% among Americans born before 1946, 58% among boomers, 50% among Gen X, and 36% among millennials, each cohort standing in emptier rooms than the last. Set that beside how couples used to meet. Church once brought couples together at something like one match in ten at its mid-century peak, and for the youngest adults it has now lost its majority as an institution at all.

It was in the middle of this draining that the phenomenon finally got its name. In 1989 the sociologist Ray Oldenburg published The Great Good Place and coined the term for what was disappearing: the third place. He defined third places as the “regular, voluntary, informal, and happily anticipated gatherings of individuals beyond the realms of home and work” — the café, pub, barbershop, or corner store where public life happens. And he was already writing their elegy. It tells you something that when Starbucks later asked him to bless its shops as third places, Oldenburg declined.

The lived experience tracks the institutional one by every soft measure available. Time-use surveys suggest Americans now spend markedly less of the day in face-to-face company than they did a couple of decades ago, and in 2023 the U.S. Surgeon General went so far as to issue a public advisory on loneliness and isolation, treating the sheer absence of social connection as a health problem in its own right. The exact magnitudes there are contested, and the trend is easier to feel than to measure cleanly, but its direction is not seriously in dispute.

Only into that emptied landscape did the app arrive. The familiar story casts dating apps as the disruptor that beat the old ways of meeting, the technology that killed the bar and the set-up-by-a-friend. The sequence runs the other way. The apps did not defeat the coffeehouse, the parish, the union hall, or the Friday dance. They moved into a vacancy those places had already left behind — family, church, and neighborhood are single-digit meeting channels now — one that had opened years earlier for reasons unrelated to any technology.

The app didn’t win the room. It inherited an empty one.

Record

Two lines from a single dataset carry the whole argument. As the physical venues emptied, one — and essentially only one — appeared to hold its ground: meeting in a bar or restaurant rose from about 19% in 1995 to 27% by 2017. Even that exception dissolves on a second look. The authors attribute the rise entirely to couples who first met online and simply had their first in-person date at a bar or restaurant; strip those out, and bars were declining as a place to meet like everything else. Every unmediated physical venue fell. And into the gap poured the app: the share of U.S. heterosexual couples who met online climbed from about 2% in 1995 to 22% in 2009 to 39% by 2017, passing friends around 2013 to become the single most common way couples meet. For same-sex couples the online share reaches about 65%.

Source — Rosenfeld, Thomas & Hausen, PNAS (2019), How Couples Meet and Stay Together.

Which venue came nearest to surviving is not a random detail, and the answer points to what the emptying really took.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, notice which venues died and which one hung on. The gathering places that vanished were built on repetition and belonging: the parish, the lodge, the union, the department-store social world, where you met the same people again and again and were vouched for by a community that knew you both. The lone apparent survivor, the bar and the restaurant, is the venue structured least like a community and most like a market — strangers, no membership, no need to know a single name, an encounter with a clean beginning and end. And even it now hangs on largely as the app’s antechamber, the place the algorithm sends its matches for a first drink. What emptied out was a whole way of meeting, not merely a set of places: repeated, low-stakes exposure among people already woven into your life, the exact opposite of the app’s model of evaluation before contact, where you appraise a stranger’s profile before a single word is exchanged.

That reframes the loneliness the apps get blamed for. The technology did not empty the room; it is what rushes in once a market has lost its venues and still needs, somehow, to clear. Which points back to where the companion essay to this one ended.

Framework

So here is the lens The Love Equations leaves you holding. Every era in this history hosted courtship in a shared physical place that existed for some other reason — water, worship, trade, company, belonging — and did its matchmaking quietly, for free, as a by-product of gathering people who had to be there anyway. The companion essay ended on the modern relationship being asked to be everything an entire village once provided. This is the floor plan of that village: the well, the churchyard, the coffeehouse, the palais, the union hall, the department-store counter where the staff cheered a courtship on by name. The Great Emptying pulled the buildings down without removing the need they served, and left the app standing in the rubble, doing alone what a whole built environment once did together.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.
