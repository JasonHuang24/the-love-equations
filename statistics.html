Statistics
By the Numbers
Where the dating market can be measured, it should be — and graded by how good the evidence is. These charts put real, sourced numbers to the asymmetries men and women actually face.

No vibes, no inflated ratios. Where the data is solid we say so; where it’s mixed we flag it; and where a viral stat breaks under scrutiny, we show the break instead of charting it. Every number traces to a primary source and carries a strength tag.

How to read the strength tags
Tier 1  Replicated research or large representative surveys. Treat as solid.
Tier 2  Real but mixed or context-dependent evidence. Directionally true.
Tier 3  Single-source, self-reported, or marketing stats. Illustrative only.

Among current or recent dating-platform users, who calls casual sex a major reason?
Men
31%

Casual sex is the one reason where men and women diverge — men out-cite it by about two and a half to one. On the other major reasons (long-term partner, casual dating, friends) Pew found no significant gender gap. So the honest story isn't "men want sex, women want love" — both rank a relationship first; men simply layer more casual openness on top. It's an overlay, not a different species.
Pew Research Center, July 2022 · N = 951 current/recent online dating users, from a survey of 6,034 U.S. adults.

Pleasure · context Tier 2
Women's odds of orgasm: casual vs. committed
Among heterosexual undergraduate women, how much does context change the payoff? (College sample — solid, but not nationally representative, so Tier 2.)
First hookup
11%
Relationship

In first-time hookups about one in nine women orgasm, versus two-thirds in committed sex. This helps explain why lower casual openness can be rational rather than prudish: when researchers held expected pleasure and anticipated stigma constant, the gender gap in accepting casual-sex offers largely vanished. Women aren't less interested in pleasure — they're less interested in the version of it that reliably underdelivers.
Armstrong, England & Fogarty (2012), American Sociological Review — women respondents; analytic Ns = 6,881 hookup / 6,591 relationship events, 21 colleges. Mechanism: Conley (2011), JPSP.

The broader gender gap persists beyond the hookup context: ~95% of heterosexual men usually orgasm versus ~65% of heterosexual women when sexually intimate. So the Armstrong chart can show that relationships improve women's odds without implying commitment fully solves the gap. Casual isn't the only place the deal is lopsided; it's just the most lopsided.
Frederick et al. (2018), Archives of Sexual Behavior — U.S. national sample, n > 52,000.

This is the demand asymmetry made personal, and it cuts both ways. Women are far more likely to feel buried in attention; men are far more likely to feel invisible for the lack of it. Same market, opposite complaints — which is also why "she feels like a body, he feels like a wallet" has a quieter cousin: she feels swarmed, he feels ignored.

No-sex-in-a-year among young adults roughly 2.5×ed in a decade — and the rise skews male. Jean Twenge's analysis of the same GSS data puts men under 30 reporting no sex in the past year near ~28% by 2018, a far steeper climb than women the same age. (The precise under-30-male figure is one analysis of the data — Tier 2, directional.) It's the attention deficit made literal: more young men aren't just feeling ignored on the market, they're falling out of it.
General Social Survey · 18–34 figures (7% → 18%) via Institute for Family Studies (Wolfinger, 2019); under-30 male figure via Twenge, GSS 2018.
Exits · who ends it Tier 2
Women file for most divorces — but not most breakups
Among heterosexual marriages that ended, who wanted the divorce?
Women
69%
Men
31%
Women initiate roughly 69% of divorces — a finding that's held across decades of court records. But the same study found a surprise: non-marital breakups split close to evenly between the sexes. So "women always leave" is really "women leave marriages more" — the gap is specific to the institution, not to women, which points at marriage carrying costs wives disproportionately feel rather than a simple branch-swing instinct.
Rosenfeld (2015/2018), "Who Wants the Breakup?" — How Couples Meet and Stay Together, nationally representative panel; via the American Sociological Association.
What people get punished for
Social judgment Tier 2
"Player" vs. "slut" — the sexual double standard
Is a man really praised for the same sexual history a woman is shamed for?
This one we deliberately don't chart with hard numbers, because the evidence is genuinely mixed. Belief in the double standard is widespread, but controlled studies often find it weaker than expected — sometimes men and women both get judged for high partner counts. Still, it bites where it counts: anticipated stigma measurably lowers women's stated willingness to accept casual sex (Conley, 2011), which means the openness gap above is probably understated. The asymmetry is real as a social perception that shapes behavior — but anyone who tells you it's been cleanly measured at some exact ratio is selling you a number that doesn't exist.
Crawford & Popp (2003) review; Marks & Fraley (2005) — findings mixed; Conley (2011) on anticipated stigma.
Numbers that sound good but break
On the cutting-room floor
Stats we refused to chart
"82% of women want relationships, 18% want casual." Traces to a single platform's self-reported marketing stat (Bumble) — Tier 3 — and it treats wanting a relationship and being open to casual as mutually exclusive, which the Pew data in the first group shows they aren't.
"75% of men are statistically left out." Built on a guessed input (≈80% of men seeking casual; Pew's solid major-reason benchmark is 31%) and a broken assumption: that casual sex pairs off one-to-one. It doesn't — that's the whole point of casual sex, and it's why the market concentrates around a minority of men rather than excluding a clean three-quarters.
