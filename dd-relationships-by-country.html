Relationships by Country

The West didn’t invent the unbundling. It was just the pilot program.

East Asia

Begin where the wave has traveled furthest. More than half the countries on Earth, holding a clear majority of the world’s people, now have fertility below the level that replaces a generation, and the line keeps moving outward. East Asia is where it has moved farthest and fastest. South Korea, Japan, Taiwan, and China have pushed their birth rates to the lowest ever recorded in peacetime, and they did it inside a single lifetime: the same retreat from marriage and childbearing the West went through, run at higher speed and against a harder economy.

One caution belongs at the very start, because it shapes every comparison in this essay. Fertility, marriage, and cohabitation are counted differently from one country to the next, and a “marriage” or a “birth outside marriage” does not mean quite the same thing in Seoul, Bogotá, and Lagos. The figures here are directional. They are close enough to line the regions up and see the shape of the thing, not precise enough to rank two similar countries by a tenth of a point.

The worry itself is not new. The oldest surviving version of it was written more than two thousand years ago, by a Greek historian watching his own country empty out.

The mechanism he named, children traded away for status and ease, is the one modern surveys still turn up when they ask people why they stopped at one child, or none. What has changed since Polybius is the scale. He was describing a few Greek cities. The same arithmetic now runs almost everywhere.

Record

South Korea recorded a total fertility rate of 0.72 in 2023 — the lowest ever logged by any country, and barely a third of the 2.1 a population needs to hold steady. Japan sits at about 1.15, China at around 1.0 on the lower estimates, and the region as a whole hovers near 1.1. At 0.72, if it held, each generation of Koreans would come in at roughly a third the size of the one before it. What keeps those fertility numbers chained to the marriage rate is how rarely the two come apart here: only about 2.4% of births in Japan and 4.7% in South Korea happen outside marriage, among the very lowest shares in the developed world, against an OECD average near 43%. Marriage and childbearing never came apart in East Asia the way they did in Europe and the Americas.

Source — Statistics Korea, 2023 vital statistics (TFR 0.72); Japan Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, 2024 (1.15); China and the regional figure via the World Bank and UN World Population Prospects 2024; births outside marriage from the OECD Family Database, SF2.4 (Japan and Korea among only five OECD countries below 10%).

The reasons are by now familiar, because they are a sharper version of the West’s. Housing in Seoul, Tokyo, and the big Chinese cities is priced beyond a young couple’s reach. Education has become an arms race that starts before kindergarten and swallows a family’s savings. Women have gained schooling, careers, and independence far faster than the domestic bargain around them has changed — so marriage still tends to mean a second shift of housework and childcare landing on the wife. The culture kept asking for a fully funded, traditionally organized marriage long after the economy that once supported one had gone.

Because marriage and birth stay so tightly tied here, the collapse of marriage in China reads straight through to its birth rate — and the register that records weddings tells that story plainly.

Record

Marriage registrations in China peaked at 13.47 million couples in 2013 and fell to 6.1 million in 2024 — more than halved in eleven years. The single-year drop into 2024 was 20.5%, leaving the annual total at its lowest in forty-six years. Part of that one-year plunge is a measurement effect: 2023 was swollen by weddings postponed during the pandemic and registered late, so 2024 is falling from an unusually high base. The eleven-year slide underneath it is no artifact.

Source — China Ministry of Civil Affairs, via Caixin (2013 peak 13.47M; 2024 total 6.1M; 2024 fall of 20.5%, a 46-year low).

It is tempting to lay China’s decline at the door of the one-child policy, and the timing rules that out. Chinese fertility began dropping in 1970, nine years before the policy arrived, falling from nearly six children per woman to under three across that decade under an earlier campaign to marry later and space births out. By the time the one-child rule was written into law, the steep part of the fall had already happened, as Our World in Data has documented at length. The policy took the credit, and later the blame, for a curve already in motion — the official lever moving only after the trend had set, a sequence that repeats across almost every country here.

The texture behind the numbers is easier to feel than to measure. Younger Koreans talk, in the press and in surveys, about giving up on dating, marriage, and children as a single package, a widely reported shorthand for a generation that increasingly treats partnering as a cost it cannot afford. The exact share who feel that way is hard to pin down and easy to sensationalize, so the survey numbers here are worth treating with real caution.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, East Asia is the cleanest test there is of a single claim. The retreat from marriage is not a Western value that spread by fashion or influence; it is what happens when a particular set of conditions arrives — expensive housing, long educations, women with their own incomes and options, and a culture still expecting a marriage to come fully funded. Wherever those conditions land, the same retreat follows, whatever the local culture says it wants. Culture can slow the wave by years or decades. Nowhere has it stopped the wave.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

Latin America

Latin America runs the experiment in almost the opposite order and arrives at the same place. Marriage here never held the monopoly it holds in East Asia. Across much of the continent, couples have for generations formed households, raised children, and stayed together without ever registering a wedding — the unión libre, the free union, a socially accepted arrangement with roots that run back centuries. Sex, cohabitation, and childbearing were only ever loosely tied to the certificate. And fertility fell below replacement all the same.

Record

The clearest measure of how far marriage and childbearing have come apart is the share of children born outside marriage, and Latin America posts the highest figures on Earth: 87% in Colombia, 78% in Chile, 74% in Costa Rica, 73% in Mexico. The average across the OECD, for comparison, is 43% today, up from just 7% in 1970. In the countries above the OECD line, a registered marriage has become close to a formality that the arrival of a child no longer requires.

Source — OECD Family Database, SF2.4, share of births outside marriage (reference year 2023); OECD average and 1970 baseline from the same source.

The fertility figures tell the same story.

Record

Brazil, the giant of the continent, now has a total fertility rate of about 1.60 — below the United States (1.62) and below France (1.64), two countries that worry openly about their own low birth rates. The only Latin American countries still clearly above replacement are a few in Central America: Guatemala at roughly 2.3, Honduras near 2.5, Nicaragua just over 2.2. Everywhere else on the continent, the line has already dipped below 2.1.

Source — UN World Population Prospects 2024.

So the region that decoupled marriage from childbearing longest ago still followed everyone else down to low fertility. A relaxed attitude to the wedding certificate bought it no extra children.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, Latin America is the mirror image of East Asia. In East Asia, marriage is close to compulsory and births happen almost only inside it. In Latin America, marriage is optional and births happen largely outside it. Two regions could hardly stand further apart in how they treat the wedding — and both have fallen below the replacement rate. If the institution can be strict or loose, central or optional, and the fertility outcome comes out the same either way, then the thing driving fertility sits underneath the marriage rule, in the economics and expectations the two regions share.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

South Asia

India poses a harder question: what happens to the birth rate when the old marriage system does not loosen at all? Marriage in India remains close to universal and, for most people, arranged. Families still vet and select partners, weigh caste and community and dowry, and broker the match; the couple’s own preference has grown but still sits inside a decision the two families largely make together. This is the alliance model the companion essay described — alive and load-bearing in the world’s most populous country. And underneath it, the birth rate has quietly slipped past the line.

Record

India’s total fertility rate has fallen to about 1.9, below the replacement level of 2.1. The estimates cluster close together: the United Nations puts it near 1.94, while India’s own National Family Health Survey measured 2.0 in 2019–21, right at the line. Either way, the world’s most populous country is no longer having quite enough children to replace itself.

Source — UN World Population Prospects 2024; National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5), 2019–21.

And it reached that point without the marriage revolution the West and East Asia went through first.

Record

A 2016 survey of Indian youth across nineteen states, run by the Lokniti programme at the Centre for the Study of Developing Societies, found that among married respondents aged 15 to 34, 84% had an arranged marriage and just 6% a love marriage; a further 4% called theirs a blend of the two. Arranged marriage, in other words, has not given way. It is still how the large majority of Indians pair.

Source — Lokniti-CSDS with the Konrad-Adenauer-Stiftung, “Attitudes, Anxieties and Aspirations of India’s Youth” (fieldwork 2016; 6,122 respondents across 19 states).

So India has reached a below-replacement birth rate with its marriage institutions still standing. That combination is what makes it such a useful test.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, India is the counter-case that rules out an easy story. If the retreat from childbearing were really about Western dating culture (apps, hookups, the fading of arranged matches), then India, where arranged marriage still dominates, ought to be immune to it. It is not. Evaluation-by-family and the matrimonial website coexist, inside the same households, with a below-replacement birth rate. The unbundling of childbearing from big families, it turns out, can run straight through a marriage system that remains, by the West’s standards, remarkably intact.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

Two things complicate the picture. Some urban surveys report arranged marriage falling fast among the young, but many of them draw on wedding-industry or big-city samples that skew toward exactly the couples most likely to have chosen each other, so the national picture is almost certainly more traditional than a fashionable metro dataset suggests. And South Asia is not a single story: Pakistan and Bangladesh sit at their own different points, on their own paths, and the Indian figures above should not be read as standing in for the whole region.

Middle East & North Africa

No country has fallen faster than Iran, and none makes the point more sharply, because Iran spent decades trying, with the full weight of the state, to steer its birth rate: first up, then down, then up again — and lost the wheel every time.

The sequence is close to a controlled experiment. During the war with Iraq in the 1980s the revolutionary state wanted soldiers and encouraged large families, and Ayatollah Khomeini spoke of raising an army of twenty million. When the war ended the government reversed itself completely: a family-planning programme relaunched at the end of the 1980s, contraception was made free and, crucially, sanctioned by clerical fatwa, and the message became ‘Fewer children, a better life.’ Fertility then fell through the floor. Alarmed at how far it had dropped, Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei reversed course once more, calling in 2011 for a population of 150 million and ordering a fresh pronatalist push. The birth rate barely responded.

Record

Iran’s total fertility rate fell from 6.6 in 1982 to 1.8 by 2002 — a drop of more than 70% in twenty years, and the fastest sustained fertility decline ever recorded. The steepest stretch, from over six children per woman to under three, took only about a decade. Iran has been below replacement since around 1999, and its own birth registrations now imply a rate under 1.5 for 2024, with fewer than a million births recorded in a single year for the first time since 1955.

Source — UN World Population Prospects 2024 and Our World in Data (the 6.6→1.8 fall and the ten-year drop); Iran’s Civil Registration Organization (2024 births below one million). The sub-1.5 figure is registration-based and runs below the UN’s own projection.

The retreat from childbearing shows up just as plainly in the retreat from the wedding itself.

Record

Registered marriages in Iran fell from a peak of about 892,000 in 2010 to roughly 481,000 in 2024, the lowest in nearly three decades. And permanent singlehood, once almost unheard of, has climbed: the share of Iranian women still unmarried at fifty rose from 1.1% in 1986 to 7.7% in 2023, a sevenfold rise inside a single generation.

Source — Iran’s National Organization for Civil Registration (marriages); Statistical Centre of Iran census data, via bne IntelliNews (never-married at fifty).

Iran is the extreme case, and it is not a lonely one. Next door, Turkey (a very different country, secular in its state and long oriented toward Europe) recorded a fertility rate of 1.48 in 2024, its eleventh straight annual decline and a record low, despite a government that has pushed hard for three children per family. Across the wider Middle East and North Africa the regional average still sits well above replacement, held up by higher-fertility countries elsewhere in the region. But its two demographic heavyweights on the northern rim, one a Shia theocracy and one a secular republic, have both fallen well below replacement.

Framework

Read through The Love Equations, Iran is the strongest evidence anywhere for where the real driver sits. Here is a government with near-total legal and religious authority over marriage and reproduction, a state that can subsidize contraception or forbid it, praise big families or penalize them, and back any of it with the moral force of the clergy. It pushed the birth rate down, and down it stayed; it pushed to bring the rate back up, and almost nothing happened. If the most powerful pronatalist instrument imaginable, a theocracy that controls the law, the money, and the pulpit, cannot lift the number on command, then the forces holding fertility low lie deeper than law and doctrine, down in how people actually live and what they can actually afford.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

The rest of the region is more mixed than Iran and Turkey alone can show, and the honest position is not to overreach. The wealthy Gulf states and the countries of North Africa sit at their own various levels, some still well above replacement, and their trajectories deserve more than a sentence borrowed from Tehran. What is clear is the direction of travel, which everywhere in the region points the same way: later marriage, smaller families, and a widening gap between what the culture expects and what the demography delivers.

Sub-Saharan Africa

End where the wave has barely arrived. Sub-Saharan Africa holds the highest fertility on Earth and the youngest populations, and it is the one large region where marriage, children, and kinship still look roughly as they did across most of human history: early and near-universal marriage, bride-wealth passing between families, and, in a wide belt across the middle of the continent, polygyny that is still openly and widely practiced.

Record

The highest-fertility countries in the world are here and almost nowhere else. Niger sits near 6.9 children per woman; Chad, Somalia, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo are all around 6.0; and rates above four are common across the region. Nearly every country with a fertility rate above four is in sub-Saharan Africa, the main exceptions being Afghanistan and Yemen. The regional average is about 4.3.

Source — UN World Population Prospects 2024.

Even here, though, the ground is shifting. Fertility has begun to slide across much of the region as girls stay in school longer and cities grow — and one measure catches the change with unusual clarity.

Record

The global adolescent birth rate (births to girls and young women aged 15 to 19) roughly halved in a single generation, falling from 74 per 1,000 in 1994 to 38 in 2024. The United Nations describes the decline as happening ‘everywhere.’ Later childbearing, the first visible step of every fertility transition in history, has now begun in the region that started it last.

Source — UNFPA, State of World Population 2025, drawing on the UN DESA Population Division, World Fertility 2024.

One rich country cuts hard against the whole pattern. Israel’s total fertility rate is 2.79 (UN WPP 2024), the developed world’s lone above-replacement outlier and close to double the OECD average of roughly 1.5, and its fertility stays high across income and education levels, running above every other rich country’s rate even among secular Israelis. Israel kept something the rest of the wealthy world let go of — the one strong hint that the decline is not quite a law of nature.

The evidence across sub-Saharan Africa is thinner than for any other region here, and these claims should be held most loosely. Trends in child marriage, the current prevalence of polygyny, and the wide gap between city and countryside are all moving and unevenly measured, and the region’s data are far thinner than East Asia’s or Europe’s. What can be said is modest and probably durable: the plateau is real, it is beginning to tilt, and the coming decades will test here what has already played out most everywhere else.

Across the five regions, a single shape resolves. The mandatory-marriage societies and the optional-marriage ones, the theocracies and the secular republics, the arranged-marriage heartland and the free-union continent have all arrived, or are arriving, at the same low-fertility destination by roads that could hardly look more different. Polybius saw the mechanism clearly enough two thousand years ago — comfort and status crowding out children. He simply saw it early, in a handful of Greek cities, when it would take another two millennia to reach the rest of us. His was a lament for a few towns. This is most of the species.

Framework

So here is the lens The Love Equations leaves you holding. The five regions are best read as one process caught at five stages: the same unbundling of sex, marriage, and childbearing that the companion essay traced through Western history, now visible as a wave crossing the whole world at different speeds. East Asia is furthest along, sub-Saharan Africa has barely begun, and the West sits between them, the region that happened to reach the turn first. The West was not the exception that broke the rule; it was the pilot program, and every other region is running the same trial a little further back on the same road.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.
