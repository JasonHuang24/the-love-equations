East Asia

Placeholder prose. Begin where the wave has traveled furthest. East Asia — South Korea, Japan, Taiwan, mainland China, Singapore — holds the lowest fertility rates ever recorded in peacetime, and it got there in a single generation. This section will argue that the region is not an exotic outlier but a preview: the same forces that loosened marriage in the West, run at higher speed and against a backdrop of intense educational competition and unaffordable housing, until pairing itself begins to look optional to a whole cohort.

Comparability caveat (placeholder). One limit belongs here at the start, because it shapes every regional comparison that follows: fertility, marriage, and cohabitation are measured differently from country to country, and a “marriage” or a “household” does not mean the same thing everywhere. Read the regional figures in this essay as directional and roughly comparable, not as identical instruments laid side by side.

The future of pairing is already on display here.

The mechanism to develop is that East Asia compressed into decades a transition the West spread across a century, and layered on top of it pressures the West never faced at the same intensity — extreme education arms races, punishing housing costs, and expectations of marriage and parenthood that stayed traditional even as the economics that once supported them fell away. The result is a widening gap between what the culture still asks of a couple and what a young person can actually afford to provide.

Framework

Placeholder Framework. Read through The Love Equations, East Asia is the clearest test of a claim this essay will make everywhere: the unbundling is not a Western value that spread, but a predictable response to a set of conditions — and wherever those conditions arrive, the same retreat from marriage follows, regardless of the local culture’s stated wishes. The lens to develop is that culture slows the wave but does not stop it.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

Latin America

Placeholder prose. Latin America complicates the story, because here the retreat from formal marriage arrived early and without ever fully passing through a golden age of it. This section will develop the region’s long tradition of informal and consensual unions, its rapid fertility decline to below replacement across much of the continent, and the way a strongly Catholic culture came to coexist with some of the world’s highest rates of cohabitation and births outside marriage.

Placeholder prose. The through-line to carry forward is that formal marriage was never the only route to family here, so its decline looks different: less a collapse than the growth of an alternative that was always partly present. The couple stayed; the certificate became optional.

The family stayed. The paperwork became optional.

The Love Equations reads Latin America as evidence that the bundle can be unbundled in a different order than the West took — sex, cohabitation, and childbearing decoupling from the marriage certificate while the pair-bond itself persists. The lens to develop: the certificate and the relationship are separable goods, and different cultures drop different pieces first.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.

South Asia

Placeholder prose. South Asia sits near the middle of the wave, and it is the section where the alliance model from the first essay is still visibly load-bearing. This section will trace near-universal, largely arranged marriage; the persistence of dowry and the joint family; and, underneath all of it, a fertility rate that has quietly fallen to around replacement even as the institution of marriage itself remains almost mandatory.

Placeholder prose. The tension to develop is between an old marriage system that still holds and a demographic reality moving fast beneath it: falling fertility, rising urban education, and a slowly growing space for the couple’s own preferences inside arrangements their families still broker.

Marriage stays universal while everything under it moves.

Middle East & North Africa

Placeholder prose. In the Middle East and North Africa the striking pattern is not the abandonment of marriage but its delay. This section will develop the phenomenon often called “waithood”: marriage remains near-universal and culturally central, yet the age at first marriage keeps climbing as young men struggle to afford the housing, bride-price, and stable income that marriage is understood to require.

Placeholder prose. The idea to develop is a bottleneck rather than a rejection. The demand for marriage stays high; the economic gate to it narrows, and a large cohort of young people ends up suspended in an extended, unwanted single adulthood they did not choose on principle.

Not a rejection of marriage — a queue for it.

Sub-Saharan Africa

Placeholder prose. End where the wave has barely begun. Sub-Saharan Africa holds the world’s highest fertility, its youngest populations, and the marriage and kinship arrangements that most of human history would recognize — early and near-universal marriage, bride-wealth, and, in a wide belt across the continent, polygyny that is still openly practiced rather than merely permitted on paper.

Placeholder prose. The argument to build is that this is not a region “behind” the others so much as a region still standing where the others started — the clearest present-day picture of the baseline the first essay described, and the place where the coming decades will test whether the same transition arrives here too, and how fast.

Not behind the others. Standing where they began.

Framework

Placeholder Framework. So here is the lens The Love Equations will leave you holding. Line the regions up by how far each has traveled and they look less like different cultures and more like different frames of one film — the same unbundling, caught at five different moments. The West was not the exception that broke the rule; it was the first frame, and everywhere else is further back on the same reel, moving forward.

That is an interpretive lens, not a documented fact — a way of reading the record, offered as such.
